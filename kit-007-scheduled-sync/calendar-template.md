# The Recurring-Review Calendar Template

Why the wording matters: in Gollwitzer & Brandstätter's 1997 study, students who merely intended to write a report finished at 32%; students who specified exactly **when and where** finished at 71%. The meta-analysis across 94 tests (Gollwitzer & Sheeran, 2006) put the overall effect at d = .65. The when/where sentence is not decoration — it is the mechanism.

## The event (copy, fill the slots, set to repeat weekly)

**Title:** `Second brain: one note, 10 minutes`

**Time:** `<a specific slot you already reliably occupy — right after an existing anchor habit beats an arbitrary time: "Tuesday, right after I make morning coffee" survives; "sometime Tuesday" dies>`

**Location:** `<the actual place: desk, kitchen table, the 8:05 train>`

**Description (paste this in — it's the implementation intention):**
```
When it is <TIME> on <DAY> and I am at <PLACE>, I will open <MY NOTES APP>,
pick ONE note (oldest untouched, or whatever this week's biggest decision
touches), and spend 10 minutes answering three lines:
1. What does this note claim?
2. What did I do differently this week because of it?
3. What will I do with it next week?
If the answer to #2 is "nothing" three weeks running, the note gets 4D'd:
Do, Date, or Delete.
```

## Rules that keep it alive

- **10 minutes means 10 minutes.** The event's job is contact, not thoroughness. An ambitious review ritual is a review ritual you'll skip.
- **Missing one is fine; rescheduling is not skipping.** When you miss, drag the event to tomorrow instead of deleting it — self-forgiveness beats shame-spiraling for habit survival, and a moved appointment is still an appointment.
- **Never make it "review my notes."** That's a goal intention (the 32% kind). The template's power is the when/where/one-note specificity.
- **Upgrade path:** once the 10-minute version has survived a month, consider size M or L from `forced-use-job-template.md` — an output with a real consumer. Don't start there.
