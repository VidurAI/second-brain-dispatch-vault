# The STRIP Checklist

The five ways a true number goes bad. Run this on any viral stat — two minutes, no agent needed. From Dispatch issue #3: of 15 audited claims from a top-1% creator, only one invented a number; five took a real number and bent it — every bend was one of these.

- **S — Swapped units.** Tokens become words. Contact rates become conversion rates. Medians become means. *Ask: is this the unit the study measured?*
- **T — Trimmed conditions.** The lab constraint vanishes ("doctors" who were barred from tools and colleagues); "in training exams" becomes "at work". *Ask: under what conditions was this measured?*
- **R — Rounded to the ceiling.** The best-case subgroup's number (800%, high-complexity roles only) becomes everyone's number. *Ask: is this the average or the ceiling?*
- **I — Inflated inference.** The study's noun survives, its verb gets upgraded: "4% of passions are job-related" → "4% can monetize their passion". *Ask: did the study test the thing the sentence now claims?*
- **P — Pinned to the wrong thing.** A control group's rate attributed to the comparison group; a competitor's benchmark score handed to the famous model. *Ask: whose number is this, exactly?*

Follow-ups that catch what STRIP misses:
1. **The anachronism check:** did the source exist when the claim was made?
2. **The meta-analysis check:** when a single study sounds too clean, search "[topic] meta-analysis" — the boring average is the number to bet on.
3. **The audit question** (credit: Sandeep Swadia): "What needs to be true for this to be real?"
